University of Ottawa

May — Sept. 2024

Research Assistant

Under Professor Arthur Mehta in the Department of Mathematics, I spent the summer studying and experimenting with non-local games. In particular, working alongside a graduate student and fellow undergraduate, we explored variants of the CHSH game. Some of that work looked like:

  • Reading notes on the mathematical foundations of quantum computing and papers pertaining to variants of the CHSH game. This involved learning about tensors, higher order functions, quantum channels, and lots more — particularly in the context of quantum computing.
  • Running 1000's of simulations of the multiplicative variants of the standard, mod-2 CHSH game as generated from roots of unity. Done in Python, this required the use of IBM's Qiskit library as well as numpy, sympy, and others to encode the components of the quantum circuits and run the simulations.
  • Preparing slides to for a presentation about quantum computing for undergraduate science students. The presentation centred around the notion of quantum advantage, and how the CHSH game proves this by way of the improvement of a quantum strategy over the optimal classical strategy.
It was a fantastic experience, and I'm thankful to have been exposed to higher-level mathematics and explore its applications in quantum computing.

Canadian Bank Note Company

Jan. — Dec. 2023

Software Developer Intern

While an intern, I had the opportunity to work on a variety of interesting and high-impact projects. Working in the manufacturing division, I was generally responsible for producing analytics and software solutions to improve quality and efficiency of operations across manufacturing at CBNC.

Some of my projects were:

  • Creating a program for production processing and product verification, used to help workers on the factory floor operate more safely and effectively. This involved conducting user interviews with 5-10 users, designing the user interface in accordance with their needs, and then writing the backend. The backend was built in Python, and involved multithreading, networking, and computer vision — exciting stuff!
  • Developing a machine learning model using density-based clustering to better predict machine failure. I leveraged my knowledge of metric spaces to define a custom metric specific to the dataset, and used that to determine a threshold across certain parameters after which machine failure was likely.
  • Creating a real-time analytics dashboard with Power BI, Python, and Azure to track and visualise sensor data. This was done in order to understand which machines were going down and when, as well as how productive each station was as part of the production line. This involved taking in data hourly from 100's of sensor inputs across 10's of machines.
  • Improving analytics dashboards pertaining to manufacturing output in Power BI by writing scripts with Power Query and Python, refactoring data table designs, and improving the usability of visualisations and tabs for stakeholders within the company.

Minitac

As part of a final project for my Great Algorithms class, myself and two group members explored the Minimax algorithm, its applications in common games and beyond, and how it compares to other algorithmic strategies such as the greedy strategy. Following the project, I developed an implementation of the Minimax algorithm in the context of tic-tac-toe, where a player tries his hand at an agent using the Minimax strategy. Collatz Numbers

In this project, I took the well-known Collatz conjecture from mathematics and used recursion to model it. I then played around with the behaviour some more, and upon visualising the behaviour of Collatz Numbers (a term I came up with to describe the number of iterations it takes for the algorithm to reach 1), I discovered the existence of "Collatz Clusters" — consecutive numbers which share the same Collatz Number. A deeper dive into the project can be found here.
